Amatka

August 15, 2017

I started reading Jagannath (a compilation of stories by Karin Tidbeck, the author) and before I realized, I was embraced by her narration style, submerged yet not drowning.
It was easy to imagine scenery, the characters and feel moved by them.

So after a couple stories I started reading Amatka1, with no knowledge whatsoever about it, no summary, no context or prior recommendation but a quote by Ann Leckie2 praising it.

The story starts with Vanja, our protagonist, a factory worker in Essre of unremarkable qualities who is tasked with travelling to Amatka and conduct a market research on hygiene products.
Up to this point other than exhibiting the good narration skills of Tidbeck, it felt dull and dizzying. I couldn’t make sense of it, had no context or legs to stand on in this book.
There is no indication that it takes part in another world, things were weird, sure, but everything described in the first few chapters fit within normal “earthy” parameters.

However, all these scattered pieces start to come together later when you get some indication that this is no longer Earth, but a colonized world.
Had I read the summary, I would not have felt so lost because you are told all of this.

From that point on, it’s a gripping story and doesn’t let go until you finish it. This reminded me of Breaking Bad, whose first season felt slow and dull too, I was really glad I pushed through it after being encouraged by friends.

Like fruit that takes time to ripen, some stories need a thorough build up before dropping you into the main course.

This is one of those.
